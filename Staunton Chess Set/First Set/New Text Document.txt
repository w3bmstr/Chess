The simplest way to size your 3D chess board and pieces is to treat everything in relative units, not real‑world units. What matters is that the pieces fit naturally on a square and look proportional from every camera angle. A clean setup uses a consistent coordinate system and a few fixed ratios.

📐 Core idea: the square defines everything
Pick a size for one board square, and all other dimensions follow from that.

A common and very stable choice is:

1.0 unit = one square width

Board = 8 × 8 units

Pieces sized relative to that 1‑unit square

This keeps your math simple and makes your engine’s movement logic line up with the 3D world.

♟️ Recommended proportions for pieces
These ratios come from real Staunton proportions, scaled to a clean digital format.

Heights (relative to square size = 1.0)
King: 1.0

Queen: 0.9

Rook: 0.7

Bishop: 0.8

Knight: 0.75

Pawn: 0.5

Base diameter (relative to square size)
All pieces: 0.4–0.5
(Most engines use 0.45 so pieces don’t touch each other.)

Clearance
Leave 0.05–0.1 units of empty space around the base so pieces don’t visually collide with the edges of the square.

🧱 Recommended board dimensions
If each square is 1×1:

Board width = 8 units

Board depth = 8 units

Board thickness = 0.2–0.3 units

Optional border frame = 0.2–0.3 units around the outside

This gives you a clean, predictable coordinate system:

Square (0,0) at bottom-left

Square (7,7) at top-right

Piece centers placed at integer coordinates (x + 0.5, y + 0.5)

🎯 Why this system works well
Your engine can place pieces by simply setting their x,y to the board coordinates.

You can scale the entire board later without breaking anything.

GLB models remain consistent across themes.

Animations (move, capture, promote) become easier because all pieces share the same origin and scale logic.

🧪 Quick example
If you choose:

Square = 1.0

King height = 1.0

King base = 0.45

Then your king model should be:

Height: 1.0 units

Base diameter: 0.45 units

Origin: centered at the base, not the middle of the mesh

Place it on e4:

Position = (4.5, 3.5, 0)

🔧 One important detail: model origin
Every piece should have its pivot/origin at the center of the base, not the bottom-left corner or the geometric center. This ensures:

Rotation looks natural

Placement on squares is trivial

Animation paths are clean
